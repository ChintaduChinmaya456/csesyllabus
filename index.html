Preliminary Examination

The Preliminary Examination consists of two papers of objective type (multiple choice questions) and carries a maximum of 400 marks. This is meant to serve as a screening test only.

Written Examination

Main Examination

The Main Examination is intended to assess the overall intellectual traits and depth of understanding of candidates rather than merely the range of their information and memory.

Standard of questions: The nature and standard of questions in GS papers (II to V) will be such that a well-educated person will be able to answer them without specialized study. Optional subject papers (VI & VII) are broadly of honours degree level.

Part A: Physical Geography

1. Geomorphology

Applied Geomorphology; Geomorphology, economic geology and environment.
2. Climatology

Types and distribution of precipitation; Weather and Climate.

Coral reefs, coral bleaching; Sea-level changes.

4. Biogeography & Env. Geography
Genesis of soils; Classification and distribution of soils; Soil profile; Soil erosion, degradation and conservation.

Principle ecology; Human ecological adaptations; Influence of man on ecology and environment.

Part B: Human Geography

1. Perspectives in Human Geography

Languages, religions and secularisation; Cultural regions of the world; Human development index.
2. Economic Geography
World economic development: measurement and problems; World resources and their distribution.
Energy crisis; the limits to growth; World agriculture: typology of agricultural regions; Agricultural inputs and productivity.
Food and nutrition problems; Food security; famine — causes, effects and remedies.
World industries: location patterns and problems; Patterns of world trade.
3. Population & Settlement Geography
Growth and distribution of world population; Demographic attributes; Causes and consequences of migration.
Concepts of over-, under- and optimum population; Population theories, world population problems and policies.

Types and patterns of rural settlements; Environmental issues in rural settlements; Hierarchy of urban settlements; Urban morphology; Concept of primate city and rank-size rule; Functional classification of towns; Sphere of urban influence; Rural-urban fringe; Satellite towns; Problems and remedies of urbanization; Sustainable development of cities.
4. Regional Planning & Models
Concept of a region; Types of regions and methods of regionalisation. Growth centres and growth poles; Regional imbalances; Regional development strategies.

Central Place theories of Christaller and Losch; Perroux and Boudeville.

Interview

Personality Test

The candidate will be interviewed by a Board who will have before them a record of his/her career. He/she will be asked questions on matters of general interest. The object of the interview is to assess the personal suitability of the candidate for a career in public service.

Total Marks
275
Counted for final merit ranking
Format
Board Interview
No fixed duration prescribed
Weight in Final
13.6%
Of the total 2025 merit marks

Purpose & Technique

The interview is a purposive conversation intended to explore the mental qualities and analytical ability of the candidate. It is not intended to be a test of either the specialised or general knowledge of the candidates, which has been already tested through their written papers.

Candidates are expected to have taken an intelligent interest not only in their special subjects of academic study but also in the events which are happening around them... in modern currents of thought and in new discoveries which should rouse the curiosity of well-educated youth.

Technique

Not a strict cross-examination but a natural, though directed and purposive conversation. The Board pays attention to the candidate's awareness of the world, balance of judgment, and capacity for social cohesion and leadership.

Qualities Evaluated

This is an assessment of not only intellectual qualities but also social traits and interest in current affairs.
